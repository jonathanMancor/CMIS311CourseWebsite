Summary

Chapter 7, "Business Skills for Technical Professionals," talks about the business skills that are increasingly necessary for technical professionals, especially as they progress in their careers. This chapter emphasizes that while basic skills such as listening, communication, and writing are foundational and can be developed on the job or through formal education, more advanced skills are required in order to progress in this career.

The chapter outlines skills that are applicable across various professions, including customer service, process management, problem-solving, project management, conflict management, and time management. It also touches on specialized skills like financial management and human resources management, which are also required later on in this career.

For those working in service desks, daily interactions with customers who experience technological issues present unique opportunities to support business objectives. Insights gained from these interactions can use to reduce incident impacts and identify areas for improvement. This requires the ability to analyze data for trends and root causes.

Moreover, the ability to communicate the benefits of improvement initiatives in financial terms using goals and metrics is also highlighted. This skill ensures that proposals are not only understood but also valued and prioritized.

The chapter also addresses the importance of presentation skills in the business world, stating that while making presentations can be challenging, they are necessary for conveying and gaining support for one's ideas. It shows how to improve these skills through practice to different settings and audiences.

Finally, it discusses the importance of advanced business skills such as project management, cost-benefit analysis, and return on investment (ROI) calculation. These skills enable technical professionals to leverage learning and growth opportunities, making themselves more competitive and making them more valued in their company. By developing these skills, technical professionals can transition from passive role players to active contributors who propose and implement solutions.

Themes:

  • Integration of business acumen with technical skills
  • Analysis techniques in business contexts (cost-benefit, ROI)
  • Project management and strategic planning

Glossary of Terms

  • Business and IT alignment: A process aimed at ensuring that information technologies support corporate goals and objectives.
  • Business case: A report that describes the business reasons that a change is being considered, along with its associated costs, benefits, and risks.
  • Business productivity team (BPT): A team that has a singular focus on improving user productivity and business outcomes (Gartner definition).
  • Change control plan: A description of how the project scope will be managed and how agreed upon changes will be incorporated into the project deliverables.
  • Considerations and concerns: A description of all considerations and concerns identified during the development of the project scope.
  • Contact volume: The total number of contacts received during a given period of time.
  • Gantt chart: A type of bar chart that is often used to illustrate a project schedule.
  • Project sponsor: The person who has ultimate authority over a project.

  • Work breakdown structure (WBS): A task-oriented breakdown of the work to be done.
