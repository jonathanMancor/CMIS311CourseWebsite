Summary

Chapter 6, "Solving and Preventing Incidents and Problems," is about the structured processes and problem-solving techniques within the role of service desk analysts to manage and resolve incidents effectively. It emphasizes the importance of the incident management process, which aims to preserve service and minimize the impact on company systems. This process is used for handling incidents consistently and efficiently.

The chapter talks about the role of the incident owner, who takes responsibility for resolving incidents within a promised timeframe and communicating status updates to customers and management. The concept of ownership is used to maintaining focus on customer needs and ensuring transparency when delays occur, preventing incidents from being overlooked and reducing customer dissatisfaction.

Effective problem-solving involves gathering data, generating information, and diagnosing the issue before implementing a solution. Effective techniques for identifying the underlying problems are asking leading questions, replicating the customer's issue, and using diagnostic tools.

Additionally, the chapter covers the problem management process, which focuses on preventing future incidents by identifying root causes. This involves detailed recording of incident data, which is essential for accurate reporting and trend analysis. Techniques like brainstorming, the Five Whys, cause and effect analysis, Pareto analysis, and Kepner-Tregoe analysis are used to thoroughly investigate and diagnose problems. The effectiveness of problem management is dependant on the accuracy of the data provided by service desk analysts.

Themes:

  • Systematic Incident Management: Exploring systematic approaches to managing the incident management process effectively.
  • Problem-solving Techniques: Covering techniques for diagnosing and resolving incidents, including the understanding of incident ownership.
  • Preventive Problem Management: Emphasizing the use of problem management processes to prevent future incidents by identifying and eliminating root causes.

Glossary of Terms

  • Cause and Effect Analysis: A technique used to generate the possible causes of a problem or effect.
  • Client/Server: A computing model where clients request services and servers respond.
  • Closed Loop Process: A process that adjusts its output based on feedback.
  • Early Life Support (ELS): The stage at the end of deployment before full operational acceptance.
  • Five Whys: Repeatedly asking "Why?" to identify the root cause of a problem.
  • Incident Data: Details of an incident or service request.
  • Incident Management: Managing the lifecycle of all incidents.
  • Incident Owner: An employee ensuring the resolution of incidents to customer satisfaction.
  • Incident Record: All fields that describe a single incident.
  • IT Planner: Responsible for IT standards and strategic alignment with business needs.
  • Kepner-Tregoe Problem Analysis: A problem-solving technique that involves defining, testing, and verifying possible causes.
  • Pareto Analysis: Identifying the most significant causes from a list of potential causes.
  • Problem Management: Managing the lifecycle of all documented problems.
  • Problem Manager: Coordinates problem management activities to meet SLA targets.
  • Request for Change (RFC): A formal request to change the production environment.
  • Resolution Data: Information describing how an incident was resolved.
  • Root Cause Analysis: Methodically determining why problems occur to prevent them.
  • Standard Change: A low-risk, preapproved change following a specific procedure.
  • Status Data: Information used to track an incident throughout its lifecycle.
  • Target Escalation Time: Time constraints ensuring incident resolution activities proceed appropriately.
